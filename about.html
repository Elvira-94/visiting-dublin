About Dublin's History

Early Period

The area that is now Dublin was settled by the Celts in the 4th century BC. The city itself was founded by the Vikings in the 9th century AD. It was a small settlement that developed around a Viking fortress. In the 12th century, the city was conquered by the Normans and became an important center of trade and commerce.

In the Middle Ages, Dublin became an important religious center and the seat of the English and later British administration in Ireland.

Middle Era

During the 17th and 18th centuries, Dublin was ruled by the British and was the second city of the British Empire after London. The city was heavily developed during this time, and many of the city's iconic buildings such as Trinity College and Dublin Castle were built during this period. Many of the city's residents at the time were Protestant and the city was a political and cultural center of the Protestant ascendancy.

During this time the population grew rapidly, many of the poor lived in tenements and the city became overpopulated.

Modern Era

In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, Dublin was a major center of the Irish literary revival, which was led by writers such as James Joyce, W.B. Yeats, and Samuel Beckett. This period also saw a resurgence of Irish nationalism sentiment, which ultimately led to the creation of the independent Irish state in 1922. Since then, Dublin has grown to become a major economic and cultural center in Ireland, with a growing reputation as a hub of technology and innovation.

In recent years, the city has undergone a significant amount of development and modernization, and today it is a vibrant and diverse city known for its rich cultural heritage, literary tradition, and warm hospitality.
